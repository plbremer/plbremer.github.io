Introduction

A large component of metabolomics analysi is identifying what chemicals are in a samble based on information such as signals (spectra) from a mass spectrometer. Classically, compounds are identified by comparing the signals of unknowns to the signals of reference standards, however, only a small fraction of compounds have reference spectra. Therefore, spectral-prediction tools such as the machine-learning/rule tool CFM-ID have been created to provide surrogate spectra for comparison.

This work benchmarks the latest CFM-ID version by 1) Looking at overall performance as a function of mass analyzer and adduct 2) Exploring performance as a function of compound 3) 4) Exploring performance as a funciton of instrument power settings

Technologies

This project made extensive use of the Python3 libraries: - pandas - sklearn - matplotlib - umap

Results Overview

Overall Performance

To measure overall performance, we first obtained compound-spectra pairs that CFM-ID was not trained on. For this, we used the NIST20 library and removed the CFM-ID training set. We then operated CFM-ID on each compound and performed a similarity test (dot product) between the experimental spectrum and the CFM-ID prediction. Then, we split the pairings according to the instrument/adduct conditions that the experiment was taken in. We histogrammed the dot product scores to yield: The convention in mass spectrometry is for a score of 999 to mean a distance of 0 and 0 to mean a distance of 1. Thus, the predictions for HCD were slightly better than those for CID, while Q-TOF performed less well.

Evaluating performance by compound.

The approach taken to evaluate performane as a function of compound is shown below.

Breifly, we started with the best and worst performing compounds from the over analysis and created a descriptor vector (called a fingerprint in cheminformatics) for each compound. We used the CACTVS/Pubchem fingerprints. We reduced the dimensionality using PCA then UMAP to create a 2-dimensional embedding. To reiterate, the locations in this space are 2-dimensional fingerprints components and the datapoints are compounds. We then histogrammed/heatmapped the reduced dimesnional space for best- and then for worst predicted compounds. We then subtracted the two heatmaps, smoothed, and provided a cutoff to create areas of descriptor-space that were expected to contain compounds with communicable trends for their prediction.

Indeed, we observed clear trends for those compounds left in the dominant spaces.

Those compounds that were better predicted were shown to be smaller and less complex.

Likewise a class-based approach provided clear compound-types for which CFM-ID could be expected to succeed.

Evaluating performance by instrument power settings

Finally, we evaluated CFM-ID performance as a function of instrument power setting. In a nutshell, as the power is increased in a mass analyzer, the degree of fragmentation increases. CFM-ID produces predictions for three power settings for 1 instrument, a Q-TOF. We show the analysis comparing both Q-TOF and HCD experimental predictions.

As expected, when the energy matches, the best performance is achieved.
