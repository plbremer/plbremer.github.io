Analytics and ML Benchmarking of CFM-ID

Introduction

Chemical identification in metabolomics analysis often relies, in part, on signals (spectra) from a mass spectrometer. Classically, compounds are identified by comparing the spectra of unknowns to a library of spectra measured from known reference standards. However, only a small fraction of compounds have reference spectra. Therefore, spectra-prediction tools, such as the machine-learning tool CFM-ID, have been created to provide predicted, surrogate spectra when experimental spectra are not available.

This work benchmarks the latest CFM-ID release by:
1) looking at overall prediction performance as a function of mass analyzer and adduct
2) exploring performance as a function of chemical identity
3) exploring performance as a function of instrument power settings

Technologies

This project made extensive use of the Python3 libraries:
* pandas
* sklearn
* umap
* matplotlib
* numpy
* snakemake

Method/Results

Overall Performance

To measure overall performance, we first obtained compound-spectra pairs that were not in the CFM-ID train/test set from the NIST20 library. We then operated CFM-ID on each compound and performed a dot-product similarity measurement between a compounds’ prediction and experimentally measured spectrum. The list of compound and similarity pairs was partitioned according to mass-analyzer and adduct and displayed below. In mass spectrometry, it is conventional to represent a similarity of 1 as 999 and a similarity of 0 as 0.

Performance by Compound

The method to evaluate performance as a function of chemical identity is outlined below:

Briefly, we started by
1. Selecting the best and worst performing compound from the overall analysis 2. We then created a descriptor vector (called a fingerprint in many chemical contexts) for each compound using the CACTVS/Pubchem fingerprints and reduced the dimensionality with PCA.
3. Unsatisfied with the differentiation, we further reduced the dimensionality with UMAP and created a 2-dimensional embedding from the 800-dimensional fingerprints.
4. To reiterate, the location in this space are 2-dimensional representations of the fingperints and each point represents a compound - color coded by best or worst prediction. We then performed two 2-dimensional histogrammings on the embedded space, one for best-predicted and one for worst predicted.
5. We then subtracted these histograms and blurred the difference.
6. We used a heigh/depth cutoff. This created locations in the embedded space where CFM-ID predicted comparatively well or comparatively poorly. We could analyze the compounds found in this space to create communicable, interpretable descriptions of when CFM-ID performs well.

To summarize our findings, we show the performance as a function of two similar metrics: molar mass and Bertz similarity (chosen because it, like spectra, does not depend on stereochemistry).

Indeed, we observed clear trends for those compounds left in the dominant spaces.

Those compounds that were better predicted were shown to be smaller and less complex.

Performance by power

Finally, we evaluated CFM-ID performance as a function of instrument power settings. This was done by histogramming the experimental spectra according to the instruments power setting at the time of recording and then displaying the average performance for each bin.

The two main results of this are that the performance is greatest when the CFM-ID power setting matches the experimental power setting (even when the power scales are orthogonal, in the case of the HCD mass analyzer) and that the lower power setting performs better overall.
